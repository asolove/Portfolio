Deco

Simple, collaborative online moodboarding. For art directors, designers, and developers.

  • User Experience
  • JavaScript
  • Node.js
  • HTML5
  • CSS3

Deco brings the benefits of design moodboards online, allowing real-time collaboration on design research and documentation. Users upload, organize, and play with images, videos, and labels until they get a feel for what the design should be.

Using example images improves client communication. Most clients don’t have the design vocabulary to describe what they want, but can quickly judge which examples fit their vision.

The Deco prototype is also a meditation on how minor advances in web technology can lead to major advances in the ease of interaction:

  • The HTML5 File API allows users to drag images from their computer into the application, rather than having to upload them in a form.
  • Multitouch, and multitouch emulation with a mouse, allow users to directly interact with their images as if they were physical objects, rather than working through dialog boxes and form controls.
  • Websockets allow extremely fast two-way communication between the server and the user's browser, making it possible to interact with no noticeable delay.

The web technology we have now is unbelievable. I designed, prototyped, built, and launched Deco, by myself, in just a month.

“What should she read?”

Parents don’t want choice, they want an answer.

  • User experience
  • Performance
  • Ruby on Rails

My wife teaches middle and high school English, so students and parents are always asking her for book suggestions. When we started building a site of her book reviews and discussion questions, we considered a lot of ways of navigating: browsing like Amazon, recommendations like Netflix, or categories like Yahoo. These systems help users make choices and emphasize the wide range of possible answers.

But parents don't want choices, they already face the thousands of young adult books in libraries and bookstores. So we simplified the site to a core dialogue. Parents ask a question and get one quality answer. If their daughter has already read that book, they can ask for another.

Because the site is a dialogue, it needs to be polite:

  • It responds quickly. The server-side code and the web page sent in reply have been meticulously tuned for speed. Only a third of a second elapses from asking a question to the answer fully-rendered on screen.
  • It doesn’t forget. The second time you visit the home page, it greets you with your most recent question and a fresh answer you’ve never seen before.
  • It is humble. It wants to recommend really good books, not brag about its extensive collection. Boys and girls mature at different rates, so it only tries to provide suggestions for girls. And when it can’t find you any more helpful answers, it apologizes and tells you where you could go to learn more.

Rare are the projects that can afford this kind of polish and this level of attention to detail. But when I see the results — girls excited to finish one book and get the next recommendation — I am inspired to spend the same care and instill the same excitement into my everyday projects.

We are not the web

Colored rectangles, form controls, drop shadows? They are not the web. I am.

  • JavaScript
  • Prototype.js
  • HTML5
  • CSS
  • Copywriting

I do a lot of work: in-house, for clients, on contract, as a volunteer. Normally someone comes to me with a plan already half thought-out. I love to solve their problems, and am glad that even people outside of the programming and design communities have established mental models of what web design can be.

But occasionally I need to stop normal work, throw away normal metaphors, and deconstruct the boxes and form controls that I try to imbue with personality. "We are not the web" is a brief interactive essay that expressed some pent-up frustration and helped me think through how to broaden my view of web design.
